Chopmarked Coins by Nationality - Guatemala
Fig. 96. 1786-NG Guatemala Portrait Eight Reales, Charles III (‘NG’ Mintmark) (KM# 36.2a). Murphy Collection.
Description:
While Guatemala introduced the new Pillar design in 1772, following the pattern of the higher-volume mints, a series of earthquakes (a frequent occurrence in Guatemala; the San Miguel earthquakes of 1717 were devastating) in 1773 almost totally eradicated the city of Santiago, and the decision was made to move the capital to Guatemala City in the aftermath. Despite an order from the Spanish Crown to evacuate, Santiago was rebuilt as the city of Antigua. Issues of 1772 and 1773 bear the ‘G’ mintmark of the earlier types, while later years (beginning in 1776) bear an ‘NG’ (‘Nueva Guatemala de la Asunción’) mintmark corresponding to the reestablished mint in Guatemala City. A heavily chopped example of the rare earlier ‘G’ mintmark variant was included as Fig. 17 of Gullberg’s Chopmarked Coins – A History.
According to a study conducted by W.F. Spalding testing the relative fineness of coins in the possession of a Chinese bank (prior to 1920), Guatemala Eight Reales of Charles III and Charles IV were not identified while those of the mints in Mexico, Peru, Bolivia, and even Chile were included. Among the rarer of the Portrait Eight Reales known with chopmarks.

Description:
The output of Nueva Guatemala mint under Charles IV almost perfectly mirrored that of the high-volume facilities in Mexico City and Bolivia in date range, but not in quantity. Like those mints, a transitional type exists bearing the likeness of the monarch’s predecessor, but only in a single naming configuration (‘Carolus IV’); the limited date range of the transitional type coupled with the mint’s scarcity makes it a rarity with chopmarks, but the ‘standard’ portrait version is far from common.

Fig. 98. 1819-NG Guatemala Portrait Eight Reales, Ferdinand VII (Bust of Ferdinand VII) (KM# 69). Murphy Collection.
(Spanish Monarchy) Ferdinand VII Portrait Eight Reales
Year(s) Issued: 1808-21
Catalog Reference(s): KM# 64 (Bust of Charles IV) (1808-10); KM# 69 (Bust of Ferdinand VII) (1808-21)
Rarity Rating: (Bust of Charles IV) R-2; (Bust of Ferdinand VII) R-3
Description:
The imprisonment of Ferdinand VII by Napoleonic forces inspired revolutionary activity in the Captaincy General of Guatemala, including independence movements in San Salvador and León as early as 1811; though quickly suppressed, these would nonetheless contribute to the political environment that resulted in the Spanish Constitution of 1812 and a series of largely informal locally autonomous governing bodies. After resuming his position as monarch in 1814, Ferdinand VII cracked down on liberal thought in Guatemala as he did elsewhere, leading to more vocal interest in independence and the 1821 Act of Independence of Central America, which formally severed Guatemala’s ties with Spain. Despite the tumultuous nature of his reign, the production of silver coinage in Guatemala remained consistent if low-volume, including a transitional type that used the portrait of Charles IV prior to the implementation of the Ferdinand VII portrait (notably, there was no attempt at improvising a portrait as there was in Mexico and Peru). Both types are rare with chopmarks; an example of the transitional was plated in Chopmarks (Fig. 45) and published with better quality images in both The Chopmark News, Vol. 15, Issue 1 (March 2011) and in Chopmarked Coins - A History (Fig. 2) as part of the Gullberg Collection.
